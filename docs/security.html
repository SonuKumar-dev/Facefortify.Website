Understanding how FaceFortify protects your data

🛡️ Security Overview

FaceFortify uses multiple layers of security to protect your files. Think of it like a safe deposit box that requires both your face (biometric) and your specific box key (hardware ID) to open.

Defense-in-Depth Approach

FaceFortify protects your data with 6 layers:

  1. Physical Security: Someone needs physical access to your computer
  2. OS Authentication: They need to log into your user account
  3. Facial Recognition: They need your face to unlock
  4. Hardware Binding: Encryption key works only on your specific computer
  5. AES-256 Encryption: Files are encrypted with military-grade encryption
  6. Access Control: Operating system blocks access to locked folders

🔐 How It Works (Simple Explanation)

Registration

When you register your face:

  1. FaceFortify takes 30 photos of your face
  2. It creates a unique "face fingerprint" (mathematical representation)
  3. This fingerprint + your computer's unique ID = encryption key
  4. The encryption key is used to lock/unlock your folders
  5. Your face photos are immediately deleted - only the fingerprint is kept
💡 Key Point: Your actual face photos are NEVER stored. Only a mathematical representation (512 numbers) is saved. Even if someone steals this data, they can't recreate your face from it.

Locking Folders

When you lock a folder:

  1. You verify your face (quick 10-second scan)
  2. FaceFortify generates the encryption key from your face + computer ID
  3. Every file in the folder is encrypted with AES-256
  4. Access permissions are removed from the folder
  5. Files are now unreadable without the correct key

Unlocking Folders

When you unlock:

  1. You verify your face again
  2. If your face matches AND you're on the same computer, the key is generated
  3. Files are decrypted back to normal
  4. Access permissions are restored
  5. You can use your files normally

🔒 Encryption Technology

AES-256 Encryption

FaceFortify uses AES-256-CBC encryption - the same standard used by:

  • 🏛️ Government agencies (NSA approved for TOP SECRET data)
  • 🏦 Banks and financial institutions
  • 🏥 Healthcare systems for patient records
  • 🔐 Password managers like LastPass and 1Password
How Strong Is It?
With AES-256, there are 2256 possible keys. That's:
115,792,089,237,316,423,....
Even if every computer on Earth (billions) tried a billion keys per second, it would take longer than the age of the universe to try them all.

Key Derivation (PBKDF2)

Your encryption key isn't just your face - it's derived through a process called PBKDF2 that:

  • Takes 100,000 mathematical operations to create the key
  • Makes brute-force attacks extremely slow (~100ms per attempt)
  • Uses a unique random "salt" for your account
  • Ensures the same face on different computers = different keys

Why 100,000 iterations? This makes each unlock attempt take about 100 milliseconds. You barely notice, but an attacker trying billions of guesses is slowed to about 10 attempts per second instead of millions.

👤 Facial Recognition Technology

ArcFace AI Model

FaceFortify uses the ArcFace model - one of the most accurate face recognition systems available:

  • Accuracy: 99%+ on standard benchmarks
  • Speed: Recognition in under 1 second
  • Works with: Different lighting, angles, expressions
  • Handles: Glasses, makeup, aging

Eye Detection

FaceFortify also detects your eyes to prevent spoofing:

  • ✅ Both eyes must be visible
  • ✅ Prevents photo-based attacks (static image)
  • ✅ Requires live face, not a picture
⚠️ Limitations: While eye detection helps prevent basic photo attacks, sophisticated attacks using high-quality 3D masks or videos could potentially work. FaceFortify is designed for personal privacy, not protection against nation-state actors.

Multi-Frame Validation

Instead of a single photo:

  • Registration: 30 frames captured
  • Verification: 10 frames captured
  • Needs 60% match rate (6/10 frames)
  • Reduces false positives/negatives

💻 Hardware Binding

What Is Hardware Binding?

Your encryption key is tied to your specific computer using:

  • CPU Serial Number: Unique processor identifier
  • Motherboard Serial: Unique board identifier
  • MAC Address: Network card identifier

These are combined and hashed to create a unique "machine ID".

Why Hardware Binding?

  • Stolen hard drive? Encrypted data is useless on another computer
  • Someone copies your face data? Still can't decrypt on their machine
  • Cloud backup of files? Can't be decrypted elsewhere

The Tradeoff

Security: Maximum protection - data works only on YOUR computer

Flexibility: If you change hardware, you need the backup password

💡 Backup Password Solution: Set a backup password that's not tied to hardware. This lets you recover access if you upgrade your computer or change major components.

🔒 Privacy & Data Storage

Complete Privacy

  • All data stays on YOUR device
  • No cloud, no servers, no internet required
  • No telemetry, no analytics, no tracking
  • No account creation, no email required
  • Open source - you can verify this yourself

What's Stored Where

Application Data Location:

  • Windows: C:\Users\YourName\AppData\Roaming\FaceFortify
  • Linux: ~/.local/share/FaceFortify
  • macOS: ~/Library/Application Support/FaceFortify

Files Stored:

  • face.dat - Your face fingerprint (512 numbers, ~2KB)
  • keyinfo.dat - Encryption metadata (salt, key hashes)
  • profile.json - Your name and settings
  • folders.json - List of locked folders
⚠️ Important: These files are encrypted and hashed, but they're still sensitive. Don't share them publicly. If you backup these files, store them securely.

✅ What FaceFortify Protects Against

  • Physical Theft: Laptop stolen? Files are encrypted and unreadable.
  • Stolen Hard Drive: Drive removed and connected to another PC? Data is encrypted and hardware-bound.
  • Other Users: Someone else logs into their account on your PC? They can't access your folders.
  • Malware/Viruses: Malicious software trying to read files? They're encrypted and access-controlled.
  • Photo-Based Attacks: Someone uses your photo? Eye detection + multi-frame validation makes this harder.
  • Unauthorized Access: Family member, roommate, colleague? Need your face to unlock.
  • Brute Force: Trying random keys? AES-256 with 2256 possibilities makes this impossible.

❌ Limitations & What We DON'T Protect Against

  • Keyloggers: Malware that captures everything you type, including your OS password, can access files when unlocked.
  • Physical Coercion: If someone forces you to unlock (the "$5 wrench attack"), FaceFortify can't help.
  • Screen Recording: Malware recording your screen can see your files while unlocked.
  • RAM Dumping: Advanced attacks that dump computer memory while files are unlocked could recover encryption keys.
  • State-Level Actors: Government agencies with unlimited resources (NSA, FBI, etc.) - this tool isn't designed for that threat level.
  • Quantum Computers (Future): Future quantum computers could theoretically break AES-256 (not a current threat).
  • Hardware Loss Without Backup: Lost your PC and didn't set a backup password? Your data is gone.
⚠️ Important Disclaimer: FaceFortify is designed for personal privacy and protection against common threats (theft, unauthorized access, casual attackers). It's NOT designed to protect against:
  • Nation-state attackers (use Tails OS + VeraCrypt)
  • Physical torture/coercion (no software can protect against this)
  • Malware with admin/root access on your system

✨ Security Best Practices

What You Should Do

  • Set a strong backup password and store it in a password manager
  • Use a strong OS/login password for your computer
  • Keep your computer locked when away (Win+L / Cmd+Ctrl+Q)
  • Install antivirus software and keep it updated
  • Keep your operating system updated with security patches
  • Only unlock folders when you need them - lock when done
  • Backup important files before encrypting (external drive or cloud)
  • Test unlock after first locking to ensure it works

What You Should NOT Do

  • Don't lock system folders (Windows, Program Files, etc.)
  • Don't share your computer password with others
  • Don't leave your computer unlocked in public spaces
  • Don't forget your backup password!
  • Don't lock folders while programs are actively using them
  • Don't manually delete files in the FaceFortify data folder

💾 Backup & Recovery

What If I Lose Access?

Scenario 1: Computer Dies

  • ✅ With backup password: Install FaceFortify on new computer, use backup password to unlock
  • ❌ Without backup password: Data is permanently lost

Scenario 2: Hardware Upgrade (new motherboard, CPU)

  • ✅ With backup password: Use backup password to unlock after upgrade
  • ❌ Without backup password: Data is permanently lost

Scenario 3: Face Not Recognized

  • ✅ With backup password: Use password instead of face
  • Try better lighting or re-register your face
⚠️ No Backdoor: There is NO master key, NO recovery service, NO way to recover data without your face+hardware OR backup password. This is intentional for maximum security, but means you MUST be responsible with your backup password!

Backup Strategy

  1. Set a backup password (store in password manager)
  2. Write it down (keep in a safe, not on your computer)
  3. Tell a trusted person (family member, lawyer - in case of emergency)
  4. Test it! Lock and unlock with backup password to ensure it works

❓ Common Security Questions

Can someone steal my face data and unlock my folders?

No. Even if someone steals your face.dat file, they would also need:

  • Your specific computer (hardware binding)
  • Your OS password to log in
  • Your face data is mathematical (can't recreate your face from it)

What if I share my computer with family?

Other users on your computer cannot access your locked folders because:

  • Your folders are encrypted with YOUR face
  • OS access control prevents other accounts from accessing
  • Each Windows/macOS/Linux user account is isolated

Is my face photo stored?

No. Photos are immediately processed into a mathematical fingerprint (512 numbers) and deleted. The original photos are never stored.

Can the government force me to unlock?

Legal questions depend on your jurisdiction. In the US, the 5th Amendment may protect against forced biometric unlocking in some cases, but this is evolving law. Consult a lawyer for specific legal advice.

What about quantum computers?

AES-256 is considered quantum-resistant for now. When quantum computers become a real threat, we'll update to post-quantum encryption algorithms.
